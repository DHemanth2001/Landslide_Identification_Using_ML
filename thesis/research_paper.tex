\section{Introduction}
Landslides are among the most terrifying and destructive natural disasters globally, typically triggered by intense rainfall, earthquakes and human activities. They result in catastrophic loss of life, severe property damage, destruction of agricultural lands, and massive economic disruption. According to the Global Fatal Landslide Database, landslides are responsible for approximately 4,500 to 5,000 people worldwide every year. India is disproportionately affected by this hazard, accounting for nearly 20\% of global landslide-related deaths, with over 12\% of the nation's total landmass classified as highly susceptible. Recent tragedies brutally underscore this ongoing threat for instance, a devastating landslide in Manipur in June 2022 killed 56 people, while the catastrophic Wayanad landslides in Kerala in 2024 claimed over 400 lives. Consequently, the rapid and accurate identification of active landslide zones plays a critical, life-saving role in modern disaster risk assessment and management.

To address this critical challenge, this project proposes an advanced, dual-phase machine learning pipeline. The goal of this study Phase 1 is to build a highly accurate system capable of detecting whether a landslide has occurred or not. Using the high-resolution optical satellite imagery provided by the Bijie Landslide Dataset, the spatial classification pipeline utilizes a weighted deep learning ensemble that combines a convolutional architecture (ConvNeXt-Base) with a vision transformer (SwinV2-Small). This detection phase heavily employs advanced optimization techniques—such as Test-Time Augmentation (TTA), and Temperature Scaling—to ensure robust, highly calibrated prediction scores that eliminate false positives.

While standard machine learning models stop at identifying present damage, the advanced and novel contribution of this project is its Phase 2 Temporal Forecasting integration. To predict future disasters, the system implements a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) to mathematically capture the stochastic, hidden state transitions of environmental disaster sequences. By geographically filtering historical, temporal, and GPS coordinate data from the NASA Global Landslide Catalog (GLC) into the HMM framework, the pipeline calculates precise emission probabilities. Ultimately, this allows the integrated application to automatically generate a 50km geographical risk map, shifting the focus from merely identifying past damage to accurately forecasting the sequence of future hazard occurrences in the target region.

Traditional algorithms fail at landslide detection because hazard zones lack simple geometric shapes and look like patches of exposed soil and broken vegetation. Deep Learning solves this by automatically extracting complex, high-dimensional visual features directly from the raw satellite imagery without needing manual human programming. It utilizes advanced mechanisms like Feature Pyramid Networks (FPN) to seamlessly recognize hazards of any size, from tiny rockfalls to massive kilometer mudslides. By leveraging over 140 million mathematical parameters, the models learn to completely ignore environmental noise like cloud shadows, atmospheric haze, and varying sun angles. Ultimately, Deep Learning acts as the powerful brain that translates satellite pixels into precise, mathematical patterns, enabling the pipeline to achieve its 98.2\% detection accuracy.

\section{Methodological Framework}

\subsection{Existing System}
In current research and disaster management protocols, the existing systems for landslide identification rely primarily on single-architecture machine learning models, such as basic Convolutional Neural Networks (e.g., AlexNet or ResNet), Support Vector Machines (SVM), or Random Forests (RF).

\textbf{Drawbacks of the Existing System:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Limited Scope (Detection Only):} Models stop entirely at visual identification without providing predictive capabilities.
    \item \textbf{Lack of Real-World Context:} Models analyze images in a vacuum without connecting to global history or future probabilities.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Proposed System}
To overcome the severe limitations of current models, this project proposes a dual-Stage Integrated Pipeline. Unlike traditional systems that only analyze the present, the proposed system dynamically connects detection with future forecasting, utilizing a ConvNeXt-Base (CBAM+FPN+TTA) and SwinV2-Small (Swin Vision Transformer V2) ensemble for Phase 1, and an HMM for Phase 2.

\subsection{Technical Components}

\textbf{1. CNN Models (ConvNeXt-Base):}
CNNs are excellent at finding local patterns (like edges, textures, and colors). It scans the satellite image to find the physical, textural signs of broken soil and vegetation.

\textbf{2. Vision Transformers (SwinV2-Small):}
While CNNs are great at looking at small, local details, Transformers are great at looking at the Global Big Picture. It connects distant parts of the image together to understand the wider context of the terrain.

\textbf{3. CBAM (Convolutional Block Attention Module):}
This is a spotlight for the CNN. Instead of the CNN treating every pixel equally, CBAM acts like a magnifying glass. It mathematically tells the CNN exactly where to look (spatial attention) and what features are most important to focus on (channel attention), completely ignoring useless background noise like clouds.

\textbf{4. FPN (Feature Pyramid Network):}
Landslides come in widely different sizes (a tiny 5-meter rockfall vs a massive 5-kilometer mudslide). FPN solves this by analyzing the image at multiple zoom levels simultaneously. It ensures the model doesn't miss a landslide just because it is too big or too small.

\textbf{5. TTA (Test-Time Augmentation):}
This is the ultimate double-check system. When the model looks at a final test image, it doesn't just look once. TTA forces the model to look at the image 5 different times (rotated, flipped, brightened, etc.) and averages the score. This guarantees the 98.2\% accuracy is real, and not just a lucky guess based on the angle of the camera.


\textbf{6. Emission Probability (Hidden Markov Model):}
We cannot see the underground stability of a mountain i.e., the Hidden State, we can only see the disasters that happen on the surface i.e., the Observations. Emission Probability is the mathematical percentage that a specific hidden underground state will emit or produce a specific visible disaster. For example: If the mountain is severely waterlogged, there is an 80\% emission probability that the next disaster will be a Mudslide.

\textbf{7. Temperature Scaling:}
Neural networks have a bad habit of being extremely arrogant. Even if model is guessing, it will often say I am 99.9\% confident. Temperature Scaling is an advanced mathematical calibration technique. It cools down the model's arrogance. After applying this, if the model says it is 90\% confident, you can mathematically prove that it is actually 90\% correct.

\textbf{Key Innovations of this Project:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A Next-Generation Ensemble (ConvNeXt + SwinV2):} Most previous research used older, single architectures like AlexNet, basic CNNs, or Random Forests. Our project uses a highly novel Ensemble mathematically combining a Convolutional Network (ConvNeXt) with a Vision Transformer (SwinV2) to achieve a massive 98.2\% accuracy.
    \item \textbf{The Integrated 2-Phase Pipeline:} Almost all previous research papers stop at Phase 1. They just say, Yes, this is a landslide, and the project ends. Our project is highly novel because it actively feeds that detection into Phase 2 (The HMM) to forecast the future. You are not just identifying the present, you are predicting the next disaster.
    \item \textbf{Automated Geographical Risk Mapping:} Instead of just outputting text, our pipeline automatically grabs the GPS coordinates from the image, connects to the NASA GLC database, and generates an automated 50-kilometer visual SVG(Scalable Vector Graphics) risk map. This makes your project an actual, deployable software tool that disaster response teams could use in the real world.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{ The Two-Stage Pipeline}
\textbf when you see at Figure 1 it is the overall Big Picture of our project. It works exactly like a security checkpoint.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Step 1:} A satellite takes a picture and hands it to the system. The model analyzes it to answer one simple question, Is there a landslide here?
    \item \textbf{Step 2:} If the model says No, the system stops. If the model says Yes, the system grabs the GPS coordinates of that picture and immediately triggers the Forecasting Engine (Phase 2) to predict what might happen next in that specific area.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{ Stage 1: Visual Hazard Recognition}
\textbf This is the phase where the system actually analyzes the picture to find the landslide. Because satellite images can be tricky (shadows, clouds, trees), relying on just one model is risky. \\
\textbf{Models Used:} We used a Ensemble, which simply means you created a team of three completely different computational models to vote on the image:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{EfficientNetV2-S (with CBAM):} It quickly scans the image. The CBAM acts like a magnifying glass, helping the model ignore useless things (like trees) and focus only on broken soil.
    \item \textbf{ConvNeXt-Base (with FPN):} This is a massive, powerful model. The FPN (Feature Pyramid Network) allows it to zoom in and out, meaning it can detect a tiny rockfall or a massive mudslide equally well.
    \item \textbf{SwinV2-Small (Vision Transformer):} While the other two models focus on small details, this model evaluates the entire landscape at once to understand the overall geography.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{The Training Algorithm:} we used techniques like Focal Loss, Mixup, and Test-Time Augmentation (TTA). Simply, You trained the system using highly altered, difficult images (Mixup) and forced the models to focus on their hardest mistakes (Focal Loss). When the system takes its final test (TTA), it analyzes the image 5 different times (upside down, rotated, etc.) to guarantee it doesn't make a lucky guess.



\subsection{ Stage 2: Statistical HMM Forecasting}
\textbf This is the chronological forecasting part of your project. Once Phase 1 finds a landslide, Phase 2 attempts to predict what disaster will happen next. \\
\textbf{Models Used:} You used a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) powered by the NASA Global Landslide Catalog. \\
\textbf We can't see the unstable soil underground (the Hidden State), we can only see the disasters that happen on the surface. The HMM acts like a statistical detective reading a history book. It analyzes over 9,000 past disasters from NASA to learn the mathematical patterns of how mountains collapse. \\
\textbf{The Algorithm (Viterbi Algorithm):} When a new landslide is found, the system uses the Viterbi Algorithm. (Note: The Viterbi algorithm is a famous, standard mathematical formula invented by Andrew Viterbi in 1967. You do not need to invent it yourself; it is a standard tool used in statistics and communications). Simply, the Viterbi algorithm takes the current landslide and traces the history backward to calculate exactly how unstable the ground is right now.




\textbf{The Final Output:} Once the system calculates the ground's instability, it generates a report detailing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What kind of physical disaster will likely happen next (e.g., Debris flow).
    \item The percentage probability of it happening.
    \item Which month it is statistically most likely to happen.
\end{itemize}

\section{Data Collection and Parameters}
The proposed dual-stage computational framework relies on two distinct, highly reliable datasets to handle both visual detection and temporal forecasting.

\textbf{1. Phase 1 Data (Classification):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset Source:} The project utilizes the Bijie Landslide Dataset, a high-quality, open-source dataset containing high-resolution optical satellite imagery of landslide-prone and non-landslide regions.
    \item \textbf{Purpose:} These images are essential to visually train the deep learning ensemble (ConvNeXt and SwinV2) to accurately distinguish between active mass wasting events and begin topographical variations.
    \item \textbf{Parameters Used:} The raw optical data (pixels) are processed as the primary input parameters. During training, the dataset is heavily modified using spatial augmentation parameters, such as geometric rotations, horizontal/vertical inversions, and Mixup/CutMix blending techniques to artificially expand the dataset and prevent model memorization.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{2. Phase 2 Data (Temporal Risk Forecasting):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset Source:} The forecasting engine is powered by the NASA Global Landslide Catalog (GLC), a comprehensive database tracking historical environmental disasters worldwide.
    \item \textbf{Purpose:} This data is used to train the Hidden Markov Model (HMM) to understand the hidden sequences and geographical patterns of how mountains collapse over time.
    \item \textbf{Parameters Used:} The system utilizes exactly 11,033 verifiable historical records. The specific parameters extracted from this dataset include:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Geospatial Parameters:} Exact GPS Coordinates (Latitude and Longitude) to draw a 50km-100km localized geographical risk boundary.
        \item \textbf{Categorical Parameters:} The specific physical failure mechanism (mapped to 7 landslide classifications, e.g., mudflow, rockfall).
        \item \textbf{Environmental Parameters:} The primary atmospheric or geological trigger (mapped to 6 specific triggers, e.g., intense rainfall, seismic activity).
        \item \textbf{Temporal Parameters:} The exact calendar dates of historical occurrences to calculate the peak statistical risk month.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
Landslides are one of the most dangerous natural disasters in the world, so it is important to detect them quickly and accurately to protect people and property. In this project, we built a smart dual-stage system to improve both landslide detection and temporal forecasting.

In the first stage, we used a combination of two powerful deep learning models (ConvNeXt and Swin Transformer) to detect landslides from images. We also used techniques like Test-Time Augmentation, Mixup, and Temperature Scaling to reduce noise and improve accuracy. Because of this, our model achieved 98.2\% accuracy on the Bijie dataset, which is 6.2\% better than earlier models. We also used Grad-CAM heatmaps to make sure the model focuses on real landslide areas instead of irrelevant background.

In the second stage, we added a prediction system using a Hidden Markov Model (HMM). When a landslide is detected, the system collects GPS location data and checks past records from NASA’s landslide database within a 50 km area. Using this data, it predicts the type, timing, and probability of future landslides.

Overall, this system not only detects landslides but also helps predict future risks, making it a useful tool for disaster management and prevention.
