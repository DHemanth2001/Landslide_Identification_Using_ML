\begin{center}{\fontsize{16}{19.2}\selectfont\bfseries Abstract}\end{center}
\vspace{5mm}
\noindent
Landslides are among the most devastating and frequently occurring natural disasters worldwide, causing thousands of deaths and billions of dollars in damage annually. Traditional landslide identification relies on manual field surveys or expert analysis of satellite imagery, both of which are slow, expensive, and cannot scale to large geographic regions. This study presents an automated two-phase machine learning pipeline for landslide identification and temporal risk forecasting using satellite imagery and historical event data.

Phase~1 employs a deep learning ensemble of three modern architectures for binary classification of satellite images into landslide and non-landslide categories. The three architectures are EfficientNetV2-S with Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM), ConvNeXt-Base with CBAM and Feature Pyramid Network (FPN), and Swin Transformer V2-Small. The system was trained and evaluated on multiple datasets including HR-GLDD and the Bijie landslide dataset, achieving 89.39\% accuracy on multi-source data (5~datasets combined) and 98.56\% accuracy on the Bijie benchmark with 5-fold cross-validation.

Phase~2 utilizes a Categorical Hidden Markov Model (HMM) trained on 9,471 real-world landslide events from the NASA Global Landslide Catalog spanning 141~countries over 28~years. Upon detection of a landslide by Phase~1, Phase~2 provides the predicted landslide type, occurrence probability, peak risk month, and a multi-step temporal forecast.

The system evolved through seven iterative experiments across three development lifecycles, improving from a baseline accuracy of 78.54\% (AlexNet from scratch) to 98.56\% (ConvNeXt-CBAM-FPN ensemble). This study demonstrates that combining spatial image detection with temporal probabilistic forecasting provides a comprehensive landslide risk assessment framework.

\chapter{Introduction}\label{intro}

\section{Background}
Landslides are one of the most destructive and frequently occurring natural disasters in the world. They cause thousands of deaths every year, destroy infrastructure, displace communities, and inflict billions of dollars in economic loss \cite{froude2018landslide}. Countries like India, Nepal, China, the Philippines, and parts of South America are particularly vulnerable because of their mountainous terrain, heavy monsoon rainfall, and rapid urban expansion into unstable slopes. India alone loses over 500 lives per year to landslides according to the National Disaster Management Authority \cite{ndma_india}.

Despite the severity of the problem, most traditional landslide identification systems rely on manual field surveys, which are slow, expensive, and impossible to scale across large geographic areas. Satellite imagery is widely available from sources such as NASA, ESA (Sentinel-2), and Landsat, but analyzing it manually by domain experts is time-consuming and not practical for real-time monitoring or early warning systems. The bottleneck in landslide identification is therefore not data availability but automated analysis capability.

Recent advances in machine learning, particularly deep learning with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers, have shown remarkable success in image recognition tasks \cite{he2016resnet, dosovitskiy2020vit}. These models can automatically learn discriminative features from raw pixel data, eliminating the need for manual feature engineering that was required by earlier approaches using Support Vector Machines or Random Forests.

However, existing deep learning approaches for landslide identification typically address only part of the problem. They can detect whether a satellite image contains a landslide but cannot classify the type of landslide, predict future occurrences, or provide temporal risk assessments. This work addresses this gap by developing a comprehensive two-phase pipeline that combines image-based spatial detection with temporal probabilistic forecasting.

\section{Problem Statement}
Every year, landslides occur in mountainous and rain-heavy regions, resulting in loss of life and property. Current methods for identifying landslides include the following.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Field surveys} involve experts physically visiting affected sites for inspection. This approach is slow, expensive, and cannot cover large areas efficiently.
    \item \textit{Expert analysis of satellite imagery} involves scientists manually examining satellite images to locate landslides. This is time-consuming and does not scale to real-time monitoring.
    \item \textit{Remote sensing indices} use methods such as NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) or change detection algorithms to flag potential landslide zones. However, they cannot classify the type or predict future events.
\end{enumerate}

The goal of this study is to develop an automated, two-phase machine learning pipeline that performs the following.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Detects landslides from satellite images with high accuracy using deep learning.
    \item Classifies the predicted landslide type based on historical regional patterns.
    \item Forecasts the probability and timing of future landslide events.
    \item Provides a complete risk assessment report for operational decision-making.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Motivation}
The motivation for this work stems from three key observations.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Scale of the problem.} Globally, landslides affect millions of people annually. Early detection and prediction could trigger evacuations and significantly reduce fatalities.
    \item \textit{Data availability gap.} Satellite imagery is abundantly available from missions like Sentinel-2, Landsat, and commercial providers. What is missing is an automated system that can analyze this imagery reliably.
    \item \textit{Limitation of existing systems.} No published system combines image-based detection with temporal forecasting. Existing approaches answer ``Is this a landslide?'' but not ``What type is it, how likely is it to recur, and when is the peak risk?''
\end{enumerate}

\section{Overview of Proposed Work}
The proposed system is a two-phase pipeline.

\textit{Phase~1, Image Classification,} takes a satellite image as input and classifies it as landslide or non-landslide using a deep learning ensemble of three modern architectures. These are EfficientNetV2-S with CBAM attention, ConvNeXt-Base with CBAM and FPN for multi-scale detection, and Swin Transformer V2-Small for global spatial understanding.

\textit{Phase~2, Temporal Forecasting,} is automatically triggered if Phase~1 detects a landslide. It uses a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) trained on 9,471 real landslide events from the NASA Global Landslide Catalog to predict the landslide type, occurrence probability, peak risk month, and a multi-step future forecast.

The system was developed iteratively through seven experiments (r0 to r6) across three development lifecycles, with each iteration addressing specific weaknesses identified in the previous one. The architecture evolved from a simple AlexNet baseline (78.54\% accuracy) to a sophisticated three-model ensemble achieving 98.56\% accuracy on the Bijie benchmark dataset.

\chapter{Literature Survey}\label{litsurvey}

This chapter reviews the existing literature on landslide detection using machine learning techniques, deep learning architectures for image classification, and temporal modeling approaches relevant to the proposed system.

\section{Traditional Landslide Detection Methods}
Traditional approaches to landslide detection broadly fall into three categories.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Remote sensing methods} use spectral indices such as NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) and change detection algorithms applied to multi-temporal satellite imagery. While effective at detecting surface changes indicative of landslides, they cannot classify the type of landslide or predict future events \cite{ghorbanzadeh2019unet}.

    \item \textit{Machine learning with handcrafted features} includes methods using Support Vector Machines (SVM), Random Forests, or Logistic Regression trained on manually engineered terrain features such as slope angle, elevation, aspect, and land cover. Zhang et al. \cite{zhang2020landslide_review} demonstrated the effectiveness of SVM and logistic regression for susceptibility mapping. However, these approaches require domain expertise for feature engineering and do not process raw image data directly.

    \item \textit{Statistical susceptibility mapping} methods use frequency ratio, weights of evidence, or information value to assess landslide susceptibility based on conditioning factors. These produce static susceptibility maps but lack temporal prediction capability.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Deep Learning for Landslide Detection}
Deep learning, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), has shown significant promise in landslide detection by automatically extracting features from satellite imagery without manual engineering.

Wang et al. \cite{wang2021landslide} compared CNNs against traditional machine learning models for landslide susceptibility mapping in Yanshan County, China. Their results showed that a basic CNN achieved 92.5\% accuracy, outperforming SVM (84\%), Random Forest (88\%), and Logistic Regression (85\%), all of which relied on handcrafted features. This demonstrated the superiority of learned features over manually engineered ones.

Ghorbanzadeh et al. \cite{ghorbanzadeh2019unet} evaluated various machine learning methods and deep learning CNNs including U-Net for landslide detection in satellite imagery. Their work demonstrated that deep CNN architectures significantly outperform traditional pixel-based classification methods.

Huang et al. \cite{huang2020landslide_dl} applied fully connected sparse autoencoders for landslide susceptibility prediction, achieving competitive results but lacking the interpretability required for operational deployment.

Meena et al. \cite{meena2022hrgldd} created the HR-GLDD (High Resolution Global Landslide Detector Dataset) containing high-resolution satellite image patches with pixel-level landslide annotations, which serves as one of the primary training datasets for the Phase~1 model in this thesis.

\section{CNN Architectures Used in This Work}
The following deep learning architectures are central to the proposed system.

\subsection{AlexNet}
AlexNet \cite{krizhevsky2012alexnet} was introduced in 2012 and consists of 5 convolutional layers followed by 3 fully connected layers, with approximately 62 million parameters. It was used as the baseline model (r0) in this thesis to establish initial performance benchmarks.

\subsection{EfficientNet}
EfficientNet \cite{tan2019efficientnet} introduced compound scaling, which uniformly scales network depth, width, and resolution using a compound coefficient. EfficientNet-B3 with 12 million parameters was used in experiments r2 to r5. The upgraded EfficientNetV2-S \cite{tan2021efficientnetv2} with fused MBConv blocks and 21 million parameters was employed in Lifecycle~3.

\subsection{ConvNeXt}
ConvNeXt \cite{liu2022convnext} is a pure CNN architecture modernized with design elements from Vision Transformers, achieving competitive accuracy. ConvNeXt-Base with 91 million parameters was used as the most powerful CNN component in the Lifecycle~3 ensemble.

\subsection{Vision Transformers}
The Vision Transformer (ViT) \cite{dosovitskiy2020vit} splits an image into fixed-size patches and processes them with a standard Transformer encoder, enabling global self-attention across the entire image. ViT-B/16 was used in the Lifecycle~2 ensemble (r5). Swin Transformer V2 \cite{liu2022swinv2} introduced hierarchical feature maps with shifted window self-attention, combining the benefits of both local and global feature extraction. SwinV2-Small with 50 million parameters was used in the Lifecycle~3 ensemble.

\subsection{Attention Mechanisms}
CBAM (Convolutional Block Attention Module) \cite{woo2018cbam} applies sequential channel and spatial attention to refine feature maps, enabling the network to focus on the most informative channels and spatial locations. FPN (Feature Pyramid Network) \cite{lin2017fpn} builds multi-scale feature maps by combining low-resolution, semantically strong features with high-resolution, spatially detailed features through a top-down pathway.

\section{Temporal Modeling with Hidden Markov Models}
Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) \cite{rabiner1989hmm} are probabilistic models for sequential data where the system transitions between hidden states that emit observable symbols. HMMs consist of three components: starting probabilities, a transition matrix governing state-to-state transitions, and an emission matrix governing observation probabilities given hidden states. Training is performed using the Baum-Welch algorithm (Expectation-Maximization), and the most likely state sequence is found using the Viterbi algorithm.

In the context of landslide forecasting, HMMs model the temporal dynamics of landslide events by treating geological regimes as hidden states and observed landslide type-trigger combinations as emissions. This approach provides interpretable probability distributions over future events, which is more informative for operational decision-making than point predictions.

Sameen et al. \cite{sameen2020lstm} compared LSTM-based models for landslide susceptibility assessment, achieving F1 scores of 0.70 to 0.80. While LSTMs can capture long-term dependencies, they suffer from limited interpretability and require substantially more training data compared to HMMs. For the scope of this work, an HMM was chosen over LSTM for its full interpretability, fast training (approximately 30 seconds compared to hours), and ability to produce complete probability distributions rather than point predictions.

\section{Calibration and Explainability}
Temperature scaling \cite{guo2017calibration} is a post-hoc calibration method that divides logits by a learned temperature parameter $T$ before softmax, ensuring that predicted probabilities match actual correctness frequencies. Grad-CAM \cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} generates visual explanations by computing gradient-weighted class activation maps, highlighting which image regions most influenced a prediction. Both techniques are incorporated in this thesis to enhance trust and operational utility.

\section{Research Gap}
Based on the literature review, the following research gap is identified. \textit{No existing system combines real-time satellite image-based landslide detection with probabilistic temporal forecasting.} Existing systems address either spatial detection or susceptibility mapping independently but do not provide an integrated pipeline that detects a landslide, classifies its type, and forecasts future events with probability estimates. This work addresses this gap through its two-phase pipeline architecture.

In summary, the literature shows that while deep learning has significantly advanced landslide detection accuracy, most existing works focus solely on spatial identification from satellite imagery. Traditional methods rely on handcrafted features and lack scalability, while modern CNN and Transformer architectures achieve high accuracy but only perform binary classification without temporal context. Temporal approaches such as LSTM-based models offer sequential modeling but lack interpretability and require large training datasets. No prior work integrates spatial image-based detection with probabilistic temporal forecasting using historical landslide catalogs. This study bridges this gap by proposing a two-phase pipeline that combines a deep learning ensemble for real-time spatial detection with an HMM-based temporal forecasting model trained on 9,471 events from the NASA Global Landslide Catalog.

\section{Overall Pipeline Architecture}\label{sec:pipeline}

If Phase~1 classifies the image as a landslide with confidence exceeding the decision threshold, Phase~2 is automatically triggered to provide temporal forecasting.

\section{Phase 1, Image Classification Architecture}\label{sec:phase1_arch}

Phase~1 evolved through three lifecycles, with each lifecycle introducing more sophisticated architectures and training strategies.

\subsection{Lifecycle 1, Foundation Models}
\textit{r0, AlexNet from Scratch.} The baseline model used AlexNet \cite{krizhevsky2012alexnet} with 5 convolutional layers, trained from random weight initialization on $227\times227$ RGB images. Binary classification into landslide and non-landslide categories using softmax with a threshold of 0.5.

\textit{r1, AlexNet with Transfer Learning.} The same AlexNet architecture initialized with ImageNet \cite{deng2009imagenet} pre-trained weights. Only the final classification layer was replaced and retrained, leveraging learned feature representations from 1.2 million images.

\textit{r2, EfficientNet-B3.} Replaced AlexNet with EfficientNet-B3 \cite{tan2019efficientnet}, featuring compound-scaled architecture with 12 million parameters. Input resolution increased to $300\times300$. Differential learning rates were applied with 0.0001 for the classification head and 0.00001 for pretrained backbone layers.

\subsection{Training Strategies}
The following training strategies were employed across the lifecycles.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Transfer Learning.} All models were initialized with ImageNet-pretrained weights to leverage learned visual feature representations.
    \item \textit{Focal Loss} \cite{lin2017focal}. This replaced the standard cross-entropy loss to address class imbalance by down-weighting easy examples as given by Equation \ref{eq:focal}.
    \begin{equation}
    \text{FL}(p) = -(1-p)^\gamma \log(p), \quad \gamma = 2.0
    \label{eq:focal}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textit{Data Augmentation.} Random horizontal/vertical flips, rotation, color jitter, Gaussian blur, and Mixup were applied to improve generalization.
    \item \textit{Test-Time Augmentation (TTA).} Five augmented views of each test image (original, horizontal flip, vertical flip, both flips, 90\textdegree{} rotation) were averaged for stable predictions.
    \item \textit{Exponential Moving Average (EMA).} This maintains a smoothed copy of model weights for more stable inference.
    \item \textit{WeightedRandomSampler.} This oversamples the minority class (landslide) during training to address dataset imbalance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Phase 2, HMM Temporal Forecasting Architecture}\label{sec:phase2_arch}

\subsection{Model Design}
Phase~2 employs a Categorical Hidden Markov Model with the specification shown in Table \ref{tab:hmm_spec}.

\begin{table}[H]
\begin{center}
\caption{Phase 2 HMM model specification.}\label{tab:hmm_spec}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\hline
{\bf Component} & {\bf Description} \\
\hline
Hidden States & 8 latent geological regimes \\
Observation Symbols & 42 (7 landslide types $\times$ 6 trigger mechanisms) \\
Transition Matrix & $8 \times 8$ state-to-state transition probabilities \\
Emission Matrix & $8 \times 42$ observation probabilities per state \\

\hline
\end{tabular}

The 7 landslide types are Landslide (generic), Debris Flow, Mudslide, Rockfall, Earth Flow, Translational Slide, and Complex Landslide. The 6 trigger mechanisms are Heavy Rain, Earthquake, Downpour, Flooding, Continuous Rain, and Other.

\subsection{Prediction Pipeline}
Given a detected landslide in a specific geographic region, Phase~2 operates as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Viterbi Decoding} determines the most likely hidden state sequence from the region's historical event observations.
    \item \textit{Type Prediction} queries the emission matrix for the current hidden state to determine the most probable landslide type.
    \item \textit{Occurrence Probability} normalizes the log-likelihood score to a $[0,1]$ range using a sigmoid function.
    \item \textit{Peak Risk Month} is computed from historical event frequency by country or region.
    \item \textit{Multi-Step Forecast} propagates the transition matrix forward $N$ steps (typically 3) to predict the sequence of most likely future landslide types with associated probabilities.
\end{enumerate}

\hline
{\bf Metric} & {\bf Description} \\
\hline
Accuracy & Percentage of correctly classified images. Can be misleading with imbalanced classes. \\
Precision & Fraction of predicted landslides that are actually landslides. Low precision indicates false alarms. \\
Recall & Fraction of actual landslides that are detected. Most critical metric for disaster monitoring as missed landslides can cost lives. \\
F1-Score & Harmonic mean of precision and recall: $F1 = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}$ \\
ROC-AUC & Area under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve. Measures the model's discriminative ability across all thresholds. \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{table}

For Phase~2 HMM, the primary metric is \textit{Log-Likelihood}, measuring how well the trained model explains the observed sequences. A higher (less negative) per-observation log-likelihood indicates better model fit.

\section{Lifecycle 1, Foundation (r0, r1, r2)}
Lifecycle~1 established the baseline and progressively improved the model architecture.

\textit{r0, AlexNet from Scratch.} The baseline model achieved 78.54\% accuracy with an F1-Score of 67.67\%. Training from random initialization with only 2,190 images proved insufficient for AlexNet's 62 million parameters, resulting in underfitting on complex landslide patterns.

\textit{r1, AlexNet with Transfer Learning.} Using ImageNet pre-trained weights improved accuracy to 80.11\% but F1-Score remained at 66.98\%, indicating that AlexNet's architectural limitations could not be overcome with better initialization alone.

\textit{r2, EfficientNet-B3.} Replacing AlexNet with EfficientNet-B3 yielded a critical improvement. While accuracy was 79.97\%, recall jumped from 67.30\% to 78.20\%, an 11 percentage point gain. This means the model caught 11\% more actual landslides, which is the most important metric for disaster monitoring. The AUC also improved from 85.73\% to 88.93\%.

\section{Lifecycle 2, Reliability Improvements (r3, r4, r5)}
Lifecycle~2 focused on calibration, ensemble methods, and architectural diversity.

\textit{r3, Temperature Calibration.} Added temperature scaling ($T = 1.1511$) to fix overconfident predictions. This did not change accuracy or F1 but ensured that when the model reports 80\% confidence, it is actually correct approximately 80\% of the time. This calibration is essential for operational trust.

\textit{r4, CNN+CNN Ensemble.} Combining EfficientNet-B3 (60\%) and AlexNet (40\%) with an optimized threshold of 0.467 improved accuracy to 82.40\% and F1 to 72.97\%. Two models making different errors resulted in more robust predictions.

\textit{r5, CNN+Transformer Ensemble.} Replacing AlexNet with ViT-B/16 in the ensemble produced the largest single improvement in the project. Accuracy rose to 84.55\%, F1 to 75.98\%, and AUC to 91.50\%. This demonstrated that \textit{architectural diversity} (CNN for local patterns combined with Transformer for global context) is more valuable than combining two architecturally similar models.

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}\label{conclusion}

\section{Conclusion}
This study presented a comprehensive two-phase machine learning pipeline for automated landslide identification and temporal risk forecasting using satellite imagery and historical event data. The methodology combines deep learning ensembles for spatial detection with probabilistic temporal modeling, addressing a critical gap in existing landslide detection systems.

In Phase~1, this work employed a deep learning ensemble of three modern architectures, namely EfficientNetV2-S with CBAM, ConvNeXt-Base with CBAM and FPN, and Swin Transformer V2-Small. The ensemble achieves 89.39\% accuracy on challenging multi-source data from five different datasets and 98.56\% accuracy on the Bijie single-source benchmark. The 5-fold cross-validation with a mean accuracy of 98.16\% and standard deviation below 0.5\% confirms the statistical reliability of these results. The system evolved through seven iterative experiments across three development lifecycles, demonstrating a total improvement of 20.02 percentage points from the initial 78.54\% baseline to the final 98.56\%. This progressive improvement validates the systematic methodology of iterative architecture selection, attention mechanism integration, multi-scale feature fusion, and ensemble composition.

In Phase~2, this work employed a Categorical Hidden Markov Model trained on 9,471 real-world landslide events from the NASA Global Landslide Catalog, spanning 141 countries over 28 years. The model provides interpretable predictions including landslide type classification, occurrence probability, peak risk month, and multi-step temporal forecasts. The per-observation log-likelihood of $-0.775$ compared to the random baseline of $-3.74$ demonstrates substantial pattern learning from the historical data.

The key contributions of this work are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A two-phase pipeline architecture that integrates spatial image detection with temporal probabilistic forecasting, representing the first such system in landslide identification literature.
    \item Systematic evaluation of six deep learning architectures across three lifecycles, demonstrating the value of architectural diversity in ensemble learning.
    \item Achievement of 98.56\% accuracy on satellite image classification, exceeding published benchmarks.
    \item Integration of attention mechanisms (CBAM), multi-scale feature fusion (FPN), and modern training strategies (Focal Loss, EMA, TTA, Mixup) for robust landslide detection.
    \item An interpretable HMM-based forecasting model trained on the largest publicly available landslide catalog.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Future Work}
The following directions are proposed for future enhancement of this system.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Pixel-level Segmentation.} Extending Phase~1 with U-Net or similar architectures for precise landslide boundary delineation rather than whole-image classification.
    \item \textit{Real-time Satellite Integration.} Connecting the pipeline to Sentinel-2 or Landsat data feeds for automated near-real-time monitoring.
    \item \textit{LSTM Ensemble for Phase~2.} Incorporating Long Short-Term Memory networks alongside the HMM for improved temporal modeling while retaining HMM interpretability.
    \item \textit{Multi-spectral Input.} Utilizing 4-channel input (R, G, B, NIR) for NDVI computation, potentially improving discrimination between landslide-affected and vegetated areas.
    \item \textit{Web Dashboard.} Building a user-facing web or mobile interface for non-technical users to upload images and receive risk assessments.
    \item \textit{Extended Environmental Features.} Incorporating rainfall data, seismic activity, soil moisture, and slope angle as additional inputs to Phase~2 for more accurate temporal predictions.
    \item \textit{Siamese Networks.} Training on multi-temporal before/after image pairs for change-detection-based landslide identification.
    \item \textit{Automated Hyperparameter Optimization.} Using Optuna or Ray Tune for systematic hyperparameter search to further optimize model performance.
\end{enumerate}
